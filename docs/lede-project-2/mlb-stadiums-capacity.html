MLB stadiums are getting smaller. Here's what's happening to all the seats.

The average MLB stadium in 2024 is over 10,000 seats smaller than the average stadium in 1994. The numbers behind this trend explain the new era of stadium construction.

By Miles Bolton

When Rogers Centre — originally known as Skydome — opened in 1989, it was a trendy, state-of-the-art multipurpose stadium that answered the calls from Toronto sports fans asking for a domed stadium to house the Blue Jays and the CFL's Toronto Argonauts. Its round concrete skeleton, connected by dimly-lit ramps, was built with the same infrastructure as many stadiums housing baseball and football across North America.

The lower level stands, divided in half, stood on tracks, meeting in one corner to fit behind a baseball diamond. When the Argonauts played, they rotated so that they ran parallel to each other, allowing a rectangular football field to run down the middle. The upper deck was built in a round shape, forming to the outer shape of the building and providing a view of both sports that didn't make sense for either — though not terrible.

Multi-purpose stadiums defined sporting venues from the 1960s to the 1990s. They made sense economically: one stadium, could be home to multiple teams, regardless of the shape of the field. These stadiums were large, most fully enclosed with seats, giving owners room to sell more tickets.

Multi-purpose stadiums were popular from the 1960s to the 1990s. They made sense economically: one stadium, could be home to multiple teams, regardless of the shape of the field. These venues were large, most fully enclosed with seats, giving owners room to sell more tickets.

In the early 1990s, MLB teams building new stadiums began shifting back to traditional ballparks, which featured stands build around a baseball field and open outfields. This shift lowered capacity, as football stadiums generally have more seats. In the following decade, more multi-purpose facilities closed, giving way for ballparks and further decreasing seating capacity across MLB.

The average capacity of an MLB stadium in 1994 was nearly 52,000. That number has decreased incrementally over the past 30 years. In 2024, the average capacity has dropped to under 42,000 — a decrease of around 20 percent.

Of the stadiums that have had their seating capacity slashed during this time, the Blue Jays have removed the most amount of seats in an effort to transform Rogers Centre into a baseball-specific venue — Tropicana Field, the home of the Tampa Bay Rays, officially has the largest capacity decrease, but this is due to the Rays closing their upper deck and covering seats with tarps, artificially reducing capacity.

After offseason renovations between the 2022 and 2024 seasons, Rogers Centre's capacity has dropped from over 50,000 to just over 39,000 — adapting to a new generation of fans and stadium trends.
